## The Shift in Attack Vectors

Historically, vulnerabilities in software or systems provided the primary attack vectors for cybercriminals. No longer. Credential theft has overtaken these traditional vulnerabilities. Now, it frequently serves as the initial point of access for attackers. Research suggests that credential-based attacks are not only easier to execute but also more effective. In fact, **68% of breaches** involve a human element, whether through phishing or stolen credentials.

Take a moment to consider how many systems in your organization rely on user credentials. Every day, employees log into various platforms, often using shared or weak passwords. ## Integration of EDR and ITDR

For many organizations, traditional EDR solutions only offer a partial view of the security landscape. While they focus on endpoint activity, they often neglect the critical identity layer — and this is where most breaches occur. Integrating EDR with Identity Threat Detection and Response (ITDR) capabilities can provide a comprehensive view of potential threats.

By monitoring identity activities such as authentication, privilege changes, and directory modifications, organizations can surface threats that EDR tools alone would miss. The ITDR market is projected to grow from **$1.2 billion in 2024 to $4.1 billion by 2028** (IDC), indicating that more companies are recognizing the importance of this integration. Unlike traditional approaches, which often treat identity and endpoints in isolation, a holistic strategy allows for real-time monitoring and faster incident response.

Consider the rise of MFA fatigue attacks, which increased **300% in 2024** (Okta Security Report 2024). If you’re relying solely on EDR, these sophisticated tactics can easily bypass your defenses. An integrated approach helps ensure that your security measures are not just reactive but proactive.
